Cribbage Rules!
About
Cribbage (herein known as Pribbage) is a 2-player card game. This particular version pits you against my programming skills, anthropomorphized as the computer. The two opponents in cribbage are refered to as the 'dealer' and the 'pone', switching positions with each deal. The object is to accumulate points faster than your opponent up to a target of either 61 or 121 points. Points are awarded for a variety of hands (including combinations of card values totalling 15, runs, flushes, and multiple cards of a given number) both during play and during the showing of the hands. A board is traditionally used to keep track of the score, with pegs being moved along a track of holes. The game has been around since sometime in the 17th century, and is a perennial favorite of pub-goers and PDA/smart-phone users everywhere.
Rules
Throw down some cards. You either know cribbage, or are smart enough to figure it out (you are using a Palm, after all). A full set of rules is beyond the scope of being included in this game, so I'll have to recommend you to some other sites...
For a comprehensive set of Official Tournament Rules, have a look at cribbage.org, the website of the American Cribbage Congress. Most rules and scoring particulars in Pribbage are based on my reading of the ACC ruleset.
The rules site at pagat.com has more succinct descriptions, and is a bit easier to read. They also have rules for 5-card cribbage.
Scoring
There are two scoring phases to each deal: the play, and the showing of the hands. The breakdown of scores are shown below (borrowed heavily from John McLeod's site).
The 'play' portion is when the dealer and pone alternate playing cards against each other to a cumulative count of 31 (based on the face value of each card). During this portion, points are awarded for a count of 15, 31, pairs, straights of three or more cards in any sequence, last card, or the inability of the opposite player to play a card without exceeding a count of 31.
15:
If you play a card which brings the running total to 15 you gain 2 points.
31:
If you play a card which brings the running total to exactly 31 you gain 2 points. The running total is then reset to zero.
Pair:
If you play a card of the same rank as the previous card you peg 2 for a pair. Note that differing face cards do not make a pair even though they are both worth 10 points.
Pair Royal:
Three cards of the same rank played in immediate succession scores 6 for a pair royal.
Double Pair Royal:
Four cards of the same rank played in immediate succession scores 12 for a double pair royal.
Run:
A run or straight is a set of 3 or more cards of consecutive ranks (irrespective of suit). The player of a card which completes a run scores for the run; the score is equal to the number of cards in the run. The cards do not have to be played in order, but no other cards may intervene.
Example: cards are played in the following order: 6-5-3-2-4. The player of the 4 scores five for a run, since that completes a run of 2-3-4-5-6.
Example:A run of 6-5-4 scores 5 points (assuming you are starting from a running total of zero), including a count of 15 for two, and a run for three.
Last Card:
If neither player manages to make the running total exactly 31, whoever played the last card in a round takes one point.
Showing of the hands brings another round of scoring, similar to play, but based solely on each players hand coupled with the cut card.
15:
Any combination of cards adding up to 15 scores two points.
Pair:
A pair of cards of the same rank score 2 points. Three cards of the same rank contain 3 different pairs and thus score a total of six points for pair royal. Four of a kind contain 6 pairs and so score 12 points for a double pair royal.
Run:
Three cards of consecutive rank (irrespective of suit), such as A-2-3, score 3 points for a run. A hand of A-2-2-3 is a double run, and scores eight points, including 2 runs of three points and a pair for two. A hand of A-2-2-2-3 (which would include the cut card) is a triple run (three runs of A-2-3 and a pair royal) and would score 15 points. A hand of A-2-2-3-3 is a double double run or a quadruple run and would score 16 points (including four runs of three and two pairs).
Flush:
If all four cards of the hand are the same suit, 4 points are scored for a flush. If the start card is the same suit as well, the flush is worth 5 points.
One For His Nobs:
If the hand contains the jack of the same suit as the start card, you peg one for his nobs.
Nineteen:
It is impossible to score nineteen in hand or in box. If you think you have, then you should either stop playing or stop drinking. Nineteen is proverbially used as a term to indicate a worthless hand.
Other potential points can be awarded, such as 'his heels' and muggins: If the start/cut card is a jack, the dealer immediately wins two points - this is called Two for his heels. Muggins is when you overlook or take too many points (e.g., with manual scoring) and the opposing player calls you out on it. The opposing player can then take the points for themself. This is not implemented in Pribbage, yet.

Statistics
There are over 12 million (12,994,800, to be exact) hands available in cribbage. The highest score possible is 29, which comes from a 5-5-5-J hand and having the cut card as the 5 of the same suit as the J.

The following table is the result of some number crunching that I did to look into how accurately (in other words, how randomly) the cards being dealt by the Pribbage code are dispersed. Additional statistical data can be found online (e.g., at Wikipedia) for comparison, although the means by which those data were obtained differs significantly from the means by which my current data is taken. The Wikipedia data (originally taken from Steve Lumetta), sequentially works its way through all possible hand combinations (a little over 12 million potential hands), and does not take into account crib selection and optimization of the hand for maximum scoring. My code, on the other hand, deals out six random cards to both dealer and pone and then applies the lumberjack discard algorithm to remove two cards from each dealt hand to the crib. The discard algorithm uses statistical analysis for optimizing the remaining cards for maximum scoring. All this is followed by randomly drawing a cut card from the remaining deck, and scoring the combined five cards in each hand. 10 million hands was chosen as an arbitrary value to ensure that the numbers converge on a final solution. There was little difference between 1 million, 10 million, and 15 million dealt hands, which suggests convergence and that this is a valid statistical sampling. At least, it does in this non-peer-reviewed assessment.
Anyway, the crib selection routine differs slightly between the dealer and pone, and both approaches have been examined. 15,000,000 pairs of hands were dealt to generate these statistics. Based on the results, there appears to be no clear bias one way or the other (i.e., to dealer or pone), and the crib distribution shows the more random distribution that matches almost exactly with the data available at Wikipedia. Pribbage approach
Pribbage uses a series of convoluted calculations and look-up tables to determine which cards to discard to the crib. The look-up tables are based on Michael Schell's discard tables and are used to enhance the calculations for maximizing the potential score for the computer. (The algorithm is also available to the player, and can be invoked by tapping the crib stack prior to selecing any cards to discard.) While the overall algorithm for discard selection is still being optimized, this appears to already provide a competitive opponent.
Selection of the computer cards during play is also accomplished through another series of convoluted computations that (in theory) are supposed to take in to account avoidance of 'needless pairs' and attempts to trap 5s (not really, but it sounds good!). This is a constantly evolving algorithm that strives to be as competitive as possible. Of course, if it gets too competitive, I've put in a preferences page with an option to change the level of play.
Finally, if the computer beats you badly and you throw your phone, please don't hold me responsible.
